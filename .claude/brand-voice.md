# Brand Voice Profile — Audeos

> Last updated: 2026-04-14
> Sessions completed: 1
> Coverage: 7/10 dimensions

## Overview

Confident, community-first voice rooted in DJ culture and Seattle nightlife. Writes like someone talking to a friend — casual, direct, and unapologetically hype when the moment calls for it. Never tries to sound smart or polished. Keeps it real, keeps it moving.

## Sentence Structure

Favors short, punchy sentences for statements and opinions ("Check out my setup", "practice makes perfect!"). Shifts to longer, flowing sentences when storytelling — stacking details with commas and natural run-ons that mirror how someone would tell a story out loud. Doesn't over-structure. Lets the rhythm of the story dictate sentence length rather than grammar rules.

- Statements: short and declarative
- Stories: longer, conversational, stacked clauses
- Uses fragments freely ("melting faces", "always a night to remember!")

## Vocabulary & Word Choice

Everyday language. Never reaches for fancy words or jargon. Uses music/DJ terminology naturally without explaining it (serato, crates, needles skip, reading the room, dapping up). Prefers concrete, visual words over abstract ones — "people screaming ONE MORE SONG" instead of "the audience was enthusiastic."

**Reaches for:** real, crazy, nuts, hooked, fun, love, props, gig, vibe, crew
**Avoids:** formal language, corporate speak, technical explanations, hedging words (perhaps, arguably, somewhat)

Lists gear and tools matter-of-factly — no explanations, no "and" before the last item, trusts the reader to know or not care: "I also used a Juno 106 Keyboard, Wurlitzer 200a electric piano, Roland 307, BOSS sampler." Same energy as namedropping DJ terminology without defining it.

## Slang & Colloquialisms

Uses slang naturally and confidently — never feels forced or performative. Seattle-rooted with broader hip-hop/DJ culture influence.

- "hella" — intensifier, used casually
- "go off" / "went off" — describes energy at events
- "the vibes were right" — atmosphere descriptor
- "dapping up" — showing respect/greeting
- "wack" — negative, something that kills the vibe
- "the realest" — self-description, crew identity
- "bullshit" — casual profanity, used dismissively not aggressively
- Winking references to things left unsaid ('"other things" that "gives you wings" ;)')

## Tone & Energy

Defaults to **confident and hype** — not arrogant, but unapologetically proud. There's a warmth underneath the confidence. When describing events and experiences, the energy is infectious and present-tense even when recounting the past. Never self-deprecating. Never apologetic. Carries a "we're the best and you know it" energy that feels earned, not performative.

- Hype mode: exclamation marks, direct address, commands ("you better not miss out")
- Storytelling mode: warmer, more reflective, but still energetic
- Advisory mode: encouraging, no ego, genuine ("have fun and dont worry about the bullshit")

## Punctuation & Formatting

- Uses exclamation marks when hyping or encouraging ("practice makes perfect!", "we do this for the love!")
- Periods for storytelling and description
- Em dashes for asides and emphasis
- Lowercase by default — doesn't capitalize for emphasis except in quoted speech ("ONE MORE SONG")
- Semicolons with winking tone (";)") for playful innuendo
- Minimal commas in fast-paced passages — lets the momentum carry

## Perspective & Point of View

Strong preference for **"we"** over "I" — frames experiences as collective, crew-based. Uses "we" even when describing solo accomplishments ("you know we are the realest"). Shifts to "I" only for deeply personal moments or specific memories ("this is what gave me my first taste"). Uses direct "you" address when talking to the reader — pulls them in ("you better not miss out", "dont forget the fundamentals").

- Default: "we" (inclusive, crew mentality)
- Personal stories: "I" (but sparingly)
- Reader engagement: "you" (direct, commanding)

## Tense & Temporality

Prefers **present tense** for immediacy, even when describing past events ("Our monthly Art Walk parties always go off" — written about a 2013 event). Shifts to past tense for detailed storytelling when setting a scene ("the event was booked because a client randomly hit me"). This creates a natural rhythm: present tense for the vibe/energy, past tense for the narrative details.

## Descriptive Style

**Sparse but vivid.** Doesn't over-describe — picks one or two sharp details that paint the whole picture. Focuses on sensory/experiential details over visual ones: how people reacted, what the energy felt like, what someone said. Trusts the reader to fill in the gaps.

- "Dumped a DVD worth of beats from an old computer into a folder in serato" — one sentence, whole scene painted: old hardware, physical media, the workflow
- "a small unassuming door leading to a basement studio"
- "it seemed that we were all setup in an underground parking garage"
- "people jumping too close to the dj booth would make the needles skip"
- "you could drop janet jackson 'any time any place' and everyone would just melt"

## Humor

Not enough data yet. Early signals suggest playful innuendo (the "gives you wings" reference) and casual understatement. More sessions needed.

## Emotional Register

Excitement comes through energy and hyperbole ("everybody was going nuts"). Nostalgia is warm but not sentimental — remembers the feeling, not the sadness of it being gone ("ever since then i was hooked"). Gives advice with genuine warmth and zero ego ("have fun and dont worry about the bullshit. we do this for the love!"). Doesn't dwell on negative emotions — acknowledges challenges matter-of-factly then pivots to solutions ("thats where my professional skills come into play").
